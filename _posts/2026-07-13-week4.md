## Week 4: Building the 30-Day Readmission Cohort and Investigating Alcohol, SDOH, and Prior Utilization

During Week 4, I made major progress in the data analysis workflow for my DREAM research project. Much of my work focused on moving from early exploratory data analysis into a more structured workflow involving cohort construction, feature engineering, statistical testing, and reproducible documentation. I used JupyterLab, pandas, Codex-assisted troubleshooting, and statistical methods to investigate how alcohol screening responses, Social Determinants of Health response patterns, and prior healthcare utilization relate to 30-day readmission risk.

One of the most important steps this week was building the 30-day readmission cohort. I identified hospital admissions and then searched for subsequent emergency department or hospital returns within a 30-day window. I also removed 2,129 admissions that did not have a full 30-day observation window. After these exclusions, the final analysis cohort included 97,310 eligible admissions. This was an important milestone because the readmission outcome is central to the rest of the project.

I also created prior-utilization measures to better understand patients’ previous healthcare use. These measures included counts of prior emergency department visits, inpatient admissions, hospital admissions, and combined ED/hospital use. I excluded same-day events from prior counts so that the measures would better reflect true prior utilization rather than multiple records from the same day. I created both preceding 365-day measures and exploratory lifetime measures. The 365-day measures are more standardized, while lifetime measures may capture more patient history but are harder to interpret because patients may have unequal amounts of recorded history.

Another major area of investigation was alcohol screening data. I compared higher versus lower alcohol use and found that higher use did not clearly predict more 30-day readmission than lower use. The patient-clustered adjusted odds ratio was 1.08 with a p-value of .558, suggesting no clear difference in readmission odds between those groups. I also compared alcohol-use evidence versus no or never alcohol-use responses. In that analysis, alcohol-use patients had approximately 21% lower adjusted readmission odds, but this should not be interpreted as alcohol being protective. A similar pattern appeared when comparing lower alcohol use versus no use, where lower-use patients had approximately 24% lower adjusted odds. These findings likely reflect other differences between patient groups rather than a causal relationship.

I also separated unclear alcohol response categories instead of treating all missing or unclear responses as the same. I distinguished no/never, declined, unable, conflicting, and indeterminate responses. This was important because different types of nonresponse may mean different things. For example, patients who decline to answer may not be the same as patients who are unable to answer. In the descriptive results, the declined group had a 12.89% readmission rate, while the unable-to-answer group had a 17.14% readmission rate. The higher descriptive rate among unable patients suggests that this group may represent a more vulnerable or medically complex population.

Prior utilization was one of the strongest signals I investigated this week. I defined high prior ED utilization as at least four emergency department visits in the preceding 365 days. Patients in this high ED-use group had about 2.6 times the readmission odds after alcohol adjustment. I also defined high prior hospital utilization as at least four hospital admissions in the preceding 365 days. These patients had about 3.7 times the readmission odds after alcohol adjustment. These findings suggest that prior utilization is an important feature to preserve in the future modeling matrix.

I also repeated the ED and hospital utilization analyses using all recorded prior history. High lifetime utilization also predicted readmission. However, I labeled these findings exploratory because patients may have different amounts of recorded history in the dataset. This helped me understand why the time window used to define a feature matters. A 365-day lookback window may be more consistent, while lifetime history may capture more information but can introduce bias if patient history is not equally available.

Another important investigation focused on Social Determinants of Health nonresponse patterns. I examined unknown, declined, unable, and incomplete or not-recorded screening responses. One finding was that declined responses predicted higher ED and hospital utilization, while unable responses predicted higher ED utilization. Unknown and incomplete/not-recorded responses showed lower recorded prior utilization, which may reflect documentation differences rather than truly lower patient need.

I also investigated SDOH nonresponse and readmission. After adjusting for other response categories and prior high ED/hospital utilization, unable responses were associated with about 19% higher adjusted odds of readmission. Incomplete or not-recorded responses were associated with about 13% higher adjusted odds. Unknown responses were associated with about 17% lower adjusted odds. Declined responses had an odds ratio of 1.12, but the p-value was .207, so this was not statistically significant. These findings showed me that missingness and nonresponse can carry information, but they need to be interpreted carefully.

The statistical approach this week included counts, percentages, chi-square tests, risk ratios, and patient-clustered logistic regression. Patient clustering was important because some patients had multiple admissions, and the analysis needed to account for repeated observations from the same person. I also used adjusted models to examine whether relationships remained after considering prior utilization and, where applicable, alcohol group.

This week helped me understand that building a machine learning dataset requires much more than simply joining files and running a model. I had to define the cohort, create the readmission outcome, engineer prior-utilization features, evaluate variable groupings, investigate missing or unclear responses, and document each step carefully. These steps are essential for reproducibility and will support future work involving Random Forest modeling, feature importance, explainability, and integration of additional data sources.

All investigations were documented in notebooks 04–06 with corresponding CSV outputs saved in the Reports folders. These findings are associations and do not demonstrate causation. Overall, Week 4 was a major step forward because I moved from early data exploration into a more complete analytic workflow for understanding hospital readmission risk.
