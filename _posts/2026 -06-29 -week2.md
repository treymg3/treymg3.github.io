During Week 2, I continued getting acclimated to the research environment, the SCHOLAR project, and the expectations of applied data science research. A major part of this week involved meeting with the Visual Informatics and Data Analytics research group at DePaul and listening to research presentations from PhD students, postdoctoral researchers, and master’s degree students.

The presentations covered a wide range of topics, including medical image analysis, curricular learning, computer vision, natural language processing, and other areas of data science and machine learning. Listening to these presentations was valuable because it gave me a better understanding of the level of professionalism, technical depth, and research communication expected in this environment. It also gave me a clearer standard to work toward as I develop my own DREAM project.

This week, I also met individually with Dr. Tchoua. During our meeting, she explained one-hot encoding and how it can be used to prepare categorical data for a machine learning model. This was helpful because many of the Social Determinants of Health variables are not numerical by default. In order for these variables to be used in a model, they often need to be transformed into a structure that the model can interpret.

We also discussed the importance of being careful when grouping cells together by SDOH domain and question type. A major concern is that grouping can make the data easier to understand, but it can also hide important details if the categories are too broad. This helped me understand that data preprocessing is not just a technical step. The way variables are grouped can affect what the model learns and whether valuable insights are preserved or lost.

My current goal is to create a report that shows whether hospital readmission is linked to whether an encounter or patient ID has high or low SDOH presence. This report will help prepare the data for a future matrix or joined table that connects encounter-level information with SDOH variables. I am working toward a structure that can show how social needs and screening responses relate to readmission outcomes.

I also began using Google and chatbox as a learning, syntax, and troubleshooting tool while working through the early data exploration process. This helped me better understand how to use pandas to inspect columns, group survey responses, count category frequencies, and think through how raw healthcare responses can be transformed into cleaner analysis-ready variables.

One specific area I explored was alcohol screening data from the Kansas population dataset. I practiced grouping response options into broader categories such as no alcohol use, low frequency, monthly frequency, weekly frequency, high frequency, and declined or unknown responses. This was an important step because raw survey responses are often too detailed, inconsistent, or difficult to interpret directly. Grouping them into meaningful categories makes the data easier to analyze and eventually easier to use in a machine learning workflow.

In my early exploration of the 30-day Kansas population readmission data, I looked at alcohol-related response groups and their counts. For the question about how often patients have a drink containing alcohol, the largest group was “No alcohol use / Never” with 34,324 responses. Other groups included low frequency with 16,988 responses, monthly frequency with 8,510 responses, weekly frequency with 5,216 responses, high frequency with 3,681 responses, and declined or unknown with 2,139 responses. This helped me see that most patients in this part of the dataset were either non-drinkers or lower-frequency drinkers, while higher-frequency alcohol use represented a smaller portion of the population.

I also reviewed the alcohol quantity question. For that question, “No alcohol use / Never” had 34,931 responses, low quantity had 26,441 responses, and moderate quantity had 4,282 responses. These early counts helped me understand the distribution of patient responses before connecting the variables to readmission outcomes. At this stage, the goal was not to make conclusions about alcohol use and readmission, but to understand the structure of the data and prepare variables for later analysis.

A major next step is to join the encounter file with the Social Determinants of Health file. The encounter file is important because it helps define the patient encounter, timing, and readmission-related outcome information. The SDOH file is important because it contains patient-level social and screening variables that may help explain readmission risk. Joining these files will require identifying the correct shared keys, checking whether the join should be based on patient identifiers, encounter identifiers, or both, and making sure that the merged dataset does not create duplicate records or lose important patients.

After joining the encounter and SDOH files, I plan to evaluate the other CSV files in the dataset as well. I want to inspect their columns, row counts, missing values, possible identifiers, and whether they contain variables that could enrich the machine learning model. This will help determine which files are useful for the current readmission prediction workflow and which files may require more cleaning before they can be integrated.

Over the weekend, I also met with the high school research team to compare research strategies and discuss early findings. This was a useful opportunity to hear how other students were approaching their work, ask questions, and think about how different research methods can be applied to the broader project.  

This week helped me understand that reproducibility is a major part of applied machine learning research. A model is not fully useful if another researcher cannot understand how the data were cleaned, which variables were included, how missing values were handled, how files were joined, how features were prepared, and how the model was evaluated. Because of this, I am paying close attention to documenting the steps in the workflow.

Overall, Week 2 helped me understand the importance of professionalism, reproducibility, and careful data preparation in research. The VIDA group presentations showed me what strong research communication looks like, my individual meeting with Dr. Tchoua clarified important machine learning preparation concepts, and my own project work helped me begin connecting data cleaning, file integration, exploratory analysis, and healthcare impact.
