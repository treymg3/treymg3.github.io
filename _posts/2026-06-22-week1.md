This week marked the beginning of my DREAM research experience with Dr. Roselyne Tchoua’s lab. To date, I had met with Dr. Tchoua a few times and began learning more about the SCHOLAR project and how it connects data-driven technologies, Community Health Worker programs, Social Determinants of Health, and hospital readmission prediction.

One important part of this first week was meeting with a subgroup team made up of high school and college researchers. The goal of this subgroup is to build research skills, develop a stronger understanding of data science, and contribute to ongoing research connected to healthcare, machine learning, and community health outcomes.

During each weekly meeting, Dr. Tchoua leads a mini lecture designed to educate the subgroup on best research practices and introduce important data science concepts. These lectures are especially helpful for me because I am coming from a non-computing background. Although I have experience in health informatics, clinical research, and healthcare quality improvement, I am still building my foundation in computer science, programming, machine learning, and data analysis.

This first week helped me understand that research is not only about getting results. It is also about asking clear questions, understanding the data, documenting the process, and learning how to communicate findings responsibly. Dr. Tchoua’s mini lecture gave helpful context for how to approach a research project with structure and professionalism. During this first week, I also spent a great deal of time getting familiar with Jupyter Lab /Notebook and realizing that pandas dataframes are more suitable than Excel analysis due to row limits, especially with the Kansas population data.

This helped me better understand why Python tools such as pandas DataFrames are more suitable than Excel for this type of analysis, especially when working with large datasets such as the Kansas population data. Excel has row limitations and can become difficult to use for larger-scale data exploration, while pandas provides more flexibility for cleaning, filtering, grouping, and analyzing healthcare data programmatically.

Working with the Kansas data is allowing me to get my feet wet with real data science techniques in a lower-pressure way before applying the workflow to the SCHOLAR project and Sinai Chicago data. This early practice is helping me understand that data science begins with careful inspection, cleaning, grouping, and interpretation of data, not just running a machine learning model.

One challenge this week was learning to be content with my incremental learning progress related to the current knowledge gaps in data science. Exploring Jupyter and referencing some of the Google Drive Folders helped steer my learning and the expectations.

My early goals for this research experience are to understand the SCHOLAR project, become familiar with the data analysis pipeline, learn the current methodology, and contribute to reproducibility of the machine learning workflow. I also want to grow in my ability to communicate and follow technical research clearly, especially in ways that connect computing methods to real healthcare problems. 

Next week, I plan to continue learning from the subgroup meetings, become more comfortable with the data science workflow, and start connecting the research methodology to my specific DREAM project objectives.
